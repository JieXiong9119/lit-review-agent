# Intake — `_example`

> **This is a worked example showing the target shape of a completed deliverable.**
> The topic is small on purpose (3 papers, ~300 words of draft) so the example
> stays readable. A real review will be 5–10× larger.

## Primary research question
"How well do current Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) approaches actually
mitigate hallucination in large language models, and where do they still fail?"

## Sub-questions
1. What are the dominant RAG architectures circa 2023–2024?
2. How is RAG performance evaluated, and what does evaluation expose?
3. When does retrieval *help* vs. *hurt* generation quality?

## Field(s) / discipline(s)
- Natural language processing
- Information retrieval

## Scope
- **Time window:** 2023–2024 (post-ChatGPT RAG era).
- **Geographic / linguistic limits:** none on authorship; English-language papers
  only.
- **Theoretical vs. applied:** both, with bias toward empirical work.
- **Other:** must address LLM-scale models (not classical seq2seq).

## Inclusion criteria
- Addresses retrieval-augmented generation directly (not generic IR or generic
  LLM work).
- Peer-reviewed venue OR widely-cited arXiv preprint (≥100 citations).
- Open-access PDF available.

## Exclusion criteria
- Pure architecture papers without empirical evaluation.
- Vendor blog posts or non-archival workshop notes.
- Work limited to encoder-only models without generation.

## Target output
- **Draft length:** ~300 words.
- **Citation count target:** 3 papers.
- **Citation key convention:** `authorYearShorttitle` (e.g., `gao2023ragsurvey`).

## Seed papers (already known to the user)
- `gao2023ragsurvey` — Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Large Language Models:
  A Survey. Gao et al., 2023. arXiv:2312.10997.
